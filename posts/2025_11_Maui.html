The Lauterbrunnen Valley doesn't ease you in. You round a bend in the road and suddenly there it is — a sheer limestone amphitheater with 72 waterfalls pouring off its walls, and the kind of silence you only get when everything around you is so enormous it absorbs sound. We had 7 days based here, and it wasn't enough.

This post covers every hike we did, what surprised us, and how we funded the whole thing with points. If you're planning a Swiss Alps trip, I hope it's useful. If you're just here for the photos — same, honestly.

The Hikes

We structured each day around a single big hike, which gave us time to actually move through the landscape instead of rushing. Here's how it broke down:

Day by Day

Northface Trail (Mürren to Grütschalp)

This was our first real day on trail and it set the bar impossibly high. The path hugs the cliff face above the valley floor with unobstructed views of the Eiger, Mönch, and Jungfrau the entire way. Low traffic, well-marked, and absolutely relentless in the best way.

Left: Looking back toward Mürren. Right: One of many meadow sections mid-trail.

Grindelwald First & Bachalpsee

We took the gondola up to First, then hiked out to Bachalpsee — a glacial lake at 2,265m that perfectly mirrors the surrounding peaks on a calm morning. We hit it around 9am before the day-trippers arrived and had it almost to ourselves. Worth every early alarm.

"Standing at Bachalpsee at 9am with the Schreckhorn reflected in the water and almost no one else around — that's the kind of moment that makes the trip planning worth it."

Männlichen Royal Walk

A gentler ridge walk with enormous payoff. The trail from Männlichen to Kleine Scheidegg is almost entirely flat and panoramic the whole way. Great option if your legs are tired from previous days — ours absolutely were.

The Royal Walk delivers wide open ridge views the entire way to Kleine Scheidegg.

The Financial Side

How We Paid For It

Switzerland is not cheap. Even with aggressive point usage, you have to go in with eyes open about daily costs. Here's a transparent breakdown of what we used and what came out of pocket.

Points & Miles Breakdown

How We Funded This Trip

Flights (SEA → OGG → SEA) 52,000 Alaska miles + $86 in fees
Hotel — Hotel Staubbach, Lauterbrunnen (6 nights) Paid cash (~$180/night)
Hotel — Helsinki (1 night) 20,000 Hyatt points
Swiss Travel Pass (rail, gondolas, buses) $260/person paid cash
Total out of pocket (2 people) ~$1,060

The Alaska miles came from a combination of the Alaska Airlines Visa welcome bonus and a Bank of America transfer. The Hyatt points were from the World of Hyatt card welcome offer. Neither required any manufactured spending — just intentional card use over about 8 months.

If you want a full post on the card strategy, I'm planning to write that separately since there's a lot to cover.

Final Thoughts

Would We Go Back?

In a heartbeat. The Bernese Oberland rewards slow travel — the more time you give it, the more it gives back. We'd skip the Swiss Travel Pass next time and buy point-to-point tickets instead, and we'd add at least two more nights to get to Oeschinensee properly. But those are good problems to have.

If you have any questions about any of this — logistics, hikes, points strategy, packing — feel free to reach out. We're happy to share what we know.
